Overview and Motivation

Our group is planning on doing our project on topics related to the NBA draft. Our primary goal is to find a way to visualize the value of an NBA draft pick. Through this goal, there are numerous opportunities visualize value in different ways. Not only are there many ways to measure a players career value, but there are many ways to visualize this data as well.

The primary motivation for our group tackling this issue is how some teams have struggled in the draft, and how some teams find diamonds in the rough. A great example of a poor selection is the Cleveland Cavaliers in 2013. The Cavaliers took Anthony Bennett with the first pick in the draft, and Bennett has spent each of his four seasons on a different team, and started only 4 games in his entire career. An example of the opposite is the last pick in the 2011 NBA Draft, Isaiah Thomas. Thomas is second in the NBA in scoring this season, and recently made his second all-star appearance. Using examples like Bennett and Thomas, our goal is to take follies and successes from the past, and turn them into visualizations. This will allow us to display clearly what is to be expected when an NBA player is taken, and subsequently how large deviations can benefit or cripple a franchise.

 

Following the submission of our first version of the project, we renewed our efforts and tried to tackle this question a different way. While we could find correlations, what seemed more interesting to us was the story where a player came from, and how that altered their successes going forward. Also, we decided clustering players would be an extremely effective way to sort out successes and failures. After defining these successes, we wanted to see how teams whod drafted well faired in the future.

 

Questions

1)    Do clusters form dividing players who play above average and below average? After learning more about clustering, we decided finding clusters of players who underperformed or over performed might be an interesting analysis.

2)    How does a players background effect their success in the NBA? Do players who find successful NBA careers come from similar collegiate backgrounds? Are they typically drafted from high school? Analyzing how player outcomes differ based on college or high school might show why some players succeed more than others.

3)    What does a good draft pick look like in the NBA? How do players who are retroactively considered to be good selections actually succeed or fail in the NBA? This question will be most likely observed in the fates of teams who draft well. Do teams who draft well end up with the best results? 

 

Data

Our data was split into two main categories: NBA data and College basketball data. NBA data was downloaded from http://www.basketball-reference.com/draft/, and college basketball data was downloaded from https://github.com/sshleifer/nbaDraft. Our NBA data ranged from 1989-2016, while our college data only covered years 2002-2006. We were sure to include data from when players could be drafted straight from high school, as well as when players could not, starting in 2006 .

 

Summary of Data

Our data was first downloaded to Excel, and then imported to Tableau for visualization.  In order to answer our questions, we needed data from before and after a player got drafted. This allowed us to make comparisons between a players college basketball performance and his NBA career. Some of the attributes that we were initially interested in were VORP (Value Over Replacement Player), points scored, 3-point percentage, and draft pick number.

Using a variety of techniques, merged our sources, added any additional columns, and cleaned the data. We used both individual season data, and player career data for the NBA players. This was done in the Basketball Reference website, by querying the data for individual seasons for each player. This added layer of granularity allowed us to use median season VORP for player evaluation. We decided this was a good measure of player ability, because it didnt overweight players with one breakout season, and didnt underweight players with long careers that may have fallen off at the end.

Cleaning the college data was necessary, as the data source was somewhat unreliable. Around three quarters of the data for college players was provided, however some players were missing. We thankfully were able to add data for the omitted rows from basketball reference, though not all columns were able to be added. This omission hurt our analysis slightly, but the information we had was still quite sufficient to provide interesting analysis.

Final Design

 

 

As you can see, the initial version of our final design was much different than the final version, and were proud of the changes weve made. The final design incorporates a redraft of players form 2002-2006, and those players are plotted and clustered with interesting results. We also analyzed the effect of your college coach (or lack thereof) and looked at just how successful each NBA team was at drafting.

The redraft was completely overhauled, and we think this version is much simpler, and informative. The far right visualization shows where a player was actually drafted, and where a player would be drafted if a redraft were held. The way the redraft was calculated was simple: the players median season VORP was sorted, and players were ranked in descending order. This allows a simple understanding of individuals in any draft who have overachieved, and let teams down.

The plot of redrafted players shows players actual selection on the x-axis, and their selection if there were a redraft on the y-axis. The size of the points is largest for the players who produced the most relative to their true draft spot, and smallest for the worst performers relative to selection. The color differences indicate four unique clusters, dividing the players from those that shine despite a low selection, and those who fail to live up to the hype of a high pick. The bottom right cluster shows the biggest over-producers, while the top left are the biggest let-downs. The bottom left cluster shows high picks who produced as expected, and the top right shows low picks who produced as expected.

The bottom visualization shows how successfully teams draft, and plots overproduction on the y-axis. Teams whose players would be consistently redrafted at a higher selection have higher averages. An interesting observation became clear: 5 of the teams in the second round of the NBA playoffs were in the top 8 of players producing higher than their draft spot. While some teams may buck this trend, Utah and San Antonio have been amongst the most successful franchises in the NBA since 2002, and placing first and third in over performing draft picks is a great example of why this occurs.  

A big improvement from our initial designs is the ability to move from career average VORP to median season VORP. While this may not seem like a big move, median season VORP turned out to be an ideal statistic to measure what we were interested in. While career average VORP is helpful, median season VORP allowed us to properly weight all seasons of a players career, by throwing out outliers. For example, a player like Grant Hill should be remembered for his years of high-flying dunks when being considered for a re-draft. Hill continued to play until he was age 40, which drives down his career average VORP in a way that isnt reflective of the way youd think of Hill if you were to draft him.
